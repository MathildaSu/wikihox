The [[Māori people|Maori]] definition also serves to acknowledge his lineage as a New Zealander. ==MahiFX As A Market Maker== The retail [[Foreign exchange market|FX]] sector is largely populated by traditional brokerage or [[ECN]]{{disambiguation needed|date=January 2013}} providers as opposed to market makers, which are more commonly found in the institutional inter-bank FX trading space. Most retail trading platform providers operate a three-tier system of trader to broker to [[market maker]]. The broker, or middle man, needs to add a layer of cost in order to make a profit. This layer of cost takes the form of a widening of the pip spread of each transaction. This accepted widespread practice of compensation for a provided service means varying degrees of higher trading execution costs for the retail trader; execution costs which banking or institutional traders are not subject to as a result of their two-tier only market-making status. The cost for executing trades is no different for an individual to a multinational. A [[market maker]] is able to connect the retail trader directly to the market instead of passing through a middle man. MahiFX, like other market makers, assumes the financial risk inherent in the transaction instead of assigning it to a third party liquidity provider. Trading through a market maker is effectively analogous to purchasing through a wholesaler instead of a department store. ==The MahiFX Platform== It is not uncommon for FX trading providers to utilise third party programs with some individualised enhancements to provide their service to retail traders. One of MahiFX’s differentiators is that its trading platform program is entirely proprietary engineered. MahiFX does not rely on any third party technology providers for their platform. ==Pre-Launch Promotion and Criticism== The pre-launch campaign for MahiFX centered on an interactive [[HTML5]] [[infographic]] hosted on the domain holding page entitled: “You vs. [[John Paulson]]”. Site visitors were invited to enter their salary into an input box and the Infographic then generated an individualised and detailed summary of the entrant’s income and purchase power relative to John Paulson, a renowned ‘super hedge fund trader’. The campaign drew mixed reviews. While many applauded the advertising concept, website design and underlying [[infographic]] technology, a minority took exception to what they considered to be an endorsement of capitalism and ostentatious spending power at a time when ‘[[Occupy Wall Street]]’ and similar movements were challenging the banking industry and culture of huge bonuses. The idea behind the campaign, according to David Cooney, was tongue-in-cheek humour around the potential available to individual traders; not a suggestion that traders could hope to achieve the level of trading success enjoyed by [[John Paulson]], through trading with the MahiFX platform.{{cite web |last=Patterson |first=Sara | title = An Interview with the Founders of MahiFX| publisher=Daily Forex| date = December 13, 2011| url = http://www.dailyforex.com/forex-figures/forex-mentor/mahifx-founders-interview/483| accessdate = April 2, 2012}} Regardless of opinion, the infographic accompanied by a public relations and general information campaign succeeded in generating attention from consumer, [[Foreign exchange market|FX]], and design websites. ==References== {{reflist}} ==Additional Information== ;Corporate Links * [http://mahifx.com Mahi FX Website] * [http://mahifx.com/john-paulson Mahi FX - John Paulson Infographic] ;Articles * {{cite web|url=http://www.prweb.com/releases/2012/2/prweb9178532.htm|title=Launch Press Release|publisher=www.prweb.com}} * {{cite web|url=http://www.dailyforex.com/forex-figures/forex-mentor/mahifx-founders-interview/483|title=An Interview with the Founders of MahiFX|first=Sara|last=Patterson|publisher=www.dailyforex.com}} [[Category:Financial services companies of New Zealand]] [[Category:Foreign exchange companies]] [[Category:Financial derivative trading companies]]
