xml:space="preserve" bytes="8364"> {{Article for deletion/dated|page=Martin MacNeill|timestamp=20131215200730|year=2013|month=December|day=15|substed=yes|help=off}} '''Martin MacNeill''' (born February 1, 1956) was convicted on November 9, 2013 of first degree [[Murder (United States law)|murder]] of his wife, [[Michele MacNeill]] on April 11, 2007. He was also convicted by the [[Provo, Utah]] jury of [[obstruction of justice]] for hindering the investigation of his wife's murder.{{cite web | url=http://www.cnn.com/2013/11/09/justice/martin-macneill-trial-verdict/ | title=Utah doctor Martin MacNeill found guilty of wife's murder | publisher=CNN | date=November 8, 2013 | accessdate=November 22, 2013 | author=Lateef Mungin}} ==Personal life== Martin and Michele MacNeill had eight children. They lived in a Salt Lake City suburb, [[Pleasant Grove, Utah]] and were members of the [[The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints]]. Martin MacNeill was the medical director of Utah State Development Center and was a bishop at The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Although MacNeill also had a law degree, he did not practice law. Several years after the death of his mother, Damien MacNeill committed suicide in January, 2010. Damien was a 24-year-old law student at the time of his death. ==Events leading up to the murder== Michele MacNeill was recovering from cosmetic surgery at the time of her death. His family stated that Michele had been reluctant to have the surgery but was pressured into the surgery by Martin MacNeill, providing the opportunity to dictate the range of prescription drugs prescribed by the cosmetic surgeon. The drugs included Valium, sleeping pills and pain killers. Prosecutors contended that MacNeill killed his wife so he could be with his mistress, Gypsy Willis. MacNeill hired Willis as a nanny for his youngest children, two weeks after murdering Michele. A few of MacNeill's daughters claimed their mother was aware of the affair and had confronted him about it before their mother's death. During the weeks leading up to the murder MacNeill gave the impression he had contracted a medical condition that required him to walk with a cane. His medical records show that he was in good health. Martin MacNeill also "had been collecting veteran benefits for decades, saying in an application he had bipolar or anti-social disorders." ==Identity theft== Martin MacNeil and Gypsy Willis were both convicted of identify theft, using the identify of MacNeill's adopted daughter for Willis' benefit. The daughter had been sent back to the [[Ukraine]]. ==Murder trial== Michele MacNeill died on April 11, 2007 as the result of a combination of [[prescription drug]]s and a [[heart disease|heart condition]]. She was found in her bathtub on the date of death. Martin MacNeill pled not guilty, claiming his wife died due to [[accidental death]]. [[Medical examiner]]s did not rule that her death was the result of murder, but prosecutors convinced the jury that Martin MacNeill was responsible for killing his wife by [[drowning]] and prescription drugs. The arrest warrant stated MacNeill had attempted murder of his mother and had killed his brother, Rufus Roy MacNeill, who had been found dead in a bathtub. MacNeill had not been tried for his death. After the prosecution filed a motion to ban camera coverage of the trial,{{cite web |url=http://fox13now.com/2013/09/27/prosecutors-want-tv-cameras-banned-from-macneill-trial/ |title=Prosecutors want TV cameras banned from MacNeill trial |publisher=FOX13Now.com |date=September 24, 2013}} in September the Utah 4th District Court, upholding a state rule which went into effect April 1, ruled that the trial could be broadcast and live-streamed - the first in Utah history. Coverage would exclude video of federal inmates who feared retaliation.{{cite web |url=http://www.sltrib.com/sltrib/news/56940300-78/macneill-trial-judge-cameras.html.csp |title=Judge: TV can live-stream coverage of Provo murder trial |work=[[The Salt Lake Tribune]] |date=September 30, 2013 }} The 22-day trial started October 18,{{cite news|url=http://nation.time.com/2013/10/18/facelift-murder-trial-underway/ |title=Facelift Murder Trial Gets Underway" |work=Time.com |date=October 18, 2013}} and was litigated by Chief Prosecutor Chad Grunander and Martin MacNeill's defense team included Randall Spencer.{{cite web | url=http://usnews.nbcnews.com/_news/2013/11/09/21381212-utah-doctor-martin-macneill-guilty-of-killing-wife-leaving-her-in-tub | title=Utah doctor Martin MacNeill guilty of killing wife, leaving her in tub | publisher=NBC | work=NBC News | date=November 9, 2013 | accessdate=November 22, 2013 | author=Paul Foy, Associated Press}} In his closing statement to the jury, Grunander stated: "'It was an almost perfect murder, [MacNeill] pumped her full of drugs' that he knew would be difficult to detect once she was dead." During the trial, a previous mistress of Martin MacNeill testified that MacNeill had stated that there was a way to kill a person and that would appear to be a natural heart attack. Fellow inmates claimed that MacNeill had claimed he killed his wife, but it couldn't be proven. The defense claimed that Michele MacNeill had overdosed on her prescribed medication and fallen into the bathtub and drowned. The jury deliberated for 11 hours and on November 9, 2013, found MacNeill guilty of both obstruction of justice and first degree murder. He is scheduled to be sentenced January 14, 2014. The murder conviction carries a term of fifteen years to life, with an additional 1-15 years for obstruction.
